SLIDE 2 — THE CHALLENGE
Retro games are built on constraints—resolutions as low as 256x240 and palettes limited to 64 colors. When you upscale these by 4x for modern displays, standard bicubic interpolation creates mud. Even modern state-of-the-art models like SwinIR fail here because they interpret sharp pixel edges as noise and smooth them out. We call this the "oil painting effect," and it destroys the artistic intent of the original creators.

---

SLIDE 3 — LITERATURE REVIEW
To understand the literature, we looked at ESRGAN—the Enhanced Super-Resolution GAN. It’s the gold standard for photorealistic upscaling, using 23 Residual-in-Residual Dense Blocks. However, its original loss functions—L1 and VGG Perceptual Loss—are trained on real-world photos. When applied to pixel art, ESRGAN still tends to anti-alias edges, which is exactly what we want to avoid.

---

SLIDE 4 — PROPOSED ARCHITECTURE
Our proposed solution was a Pixel-Aware RRDBNet. We stripped the RRDB trunk down to 8 blocks to avoid overfitting on the simpler geometry of pixel art and replaced the sub-pixel convolution upsampler with a Nearest-Neighbor interpolation layer. This geometric prior ensures that the network starts with a blocky baseline rather than a blurry one.

---

SLIDE 5 — FINAL MODEL & EVOLUTION
In our final implementation for Milestone 2, we introduced a Palette-Constraint Loss. This is a custom penalty that snaps generated color values to discrete 8-bit grids. This solved the "color drifting" issue where the GAN would invent new shades not present in the source. Our final objective function is a weighted combination of L1, LPIPS perceptual loss, our Sobel-based Edge-Aware loss, the new Palette loss, and a fully enabled Adversarial Loss.

---

SLIDE 6 — TRAINING RESULTS
We scaled our training to the full 89,000-image Kaggle Pixel Art dataset. Over 20 epochs, we saw a clear convergence where the generator successfully "fooled" the discriminator. As shown in the charts, our adversarial component drove the high-frequency sharpness while the custom edge and palette losses kept the output aligned with the pixel grid.

ANTICIPATED QUESTIONS & ANSWERS:
Q: Why use only 8 RRDB blocks?
A: Pixel art has lower entropy and more repetitive geometric patterns than natural images. 8 blocks provided the perfect balance between reconstruction quality and inference speed, avoiding the "over-smoothing" we saw with 23 blocks.

Q: How do you handle colorkeys/transparency in the demo?
A: We implemented a "near-black" cleanup function that snaps ESRGAN's slightly noisy output back to pure (0,0,0) so Pygame's transparency masking remains perfect.

Q: Is the 79x improvement just because you used a better dataset?
A: No, the 79x is measured on LPIPS (perceptual similarity). The improvement comes from the model learning to reconstruct sharp edges rather than blurry approximations.
