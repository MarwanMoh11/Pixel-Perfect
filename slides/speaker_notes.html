Context
"Our project uses an enhanced version of ESRGAN with two key modifications — nearest-neighbor downsampling and a custom edge-aware sharpness loss — to achieve a 4× upscale of pixel art while preserving its unique visual style."
2
The Problem
▾
Say This
"Retro games like NES and SNES run at extremely low resolutions — around 256 by 240 pixels. When you display these on a modern 4K monitor, they need to be upscaled massively."
"Standard methods like bicubic interpolation just produce blurry, muddy results. And here's the interesting part — even state-of-the-art super-resolution models like SRGAN and SwinIR fail on pixel art. They're trained on photos, so they interpret those sharp pixel edges as noise and smooth them out. You get this 'oil painting' effect that completely destroys the artistic intent."
"What we need instead is a model that respects pixel art geometry — hard edges, limited palettes, grid-aligned blocks. That's what Pixel-Perfect is designed to do."
3
Dataset & Preprocessing
▾
Say This
"For our dataset, we selected the Kaggle Pixel Art Dataset — it contains about 89,000 pixel-art images of sprites and game objects, all at 16 by 16 resolution."
"We split this 80/10/10 for training, validation, and testing. The dataset is around 500 megabytes, which is well within Colab's capacity."
"A critical part of our preprocessing is the nearest-neighbor downsampling. We resize the sprites to 128 by 128 using NN interpolation to create our high-res targets, then downsample to 32 by 32 — also using NN — to create the low-res inputs. This is important because it preserves the blocky geometry that defines pixel art, unlike bicubic which introduces blur."
💡 Point to the chart: "As you can see from the statistics, the vast majority of our data is in the training set."

5
Model Architecture
▾
Say This
"Our model is based on ESRGAN — Enhanced Super-Resolution GAN. The generator uses the RRDBNet architecture — Residual-in-Residual Dense Block Network."
"The flow is: a 32 by 32 low-res image enters the network, passes through an initial convolution, then through 8 RRDB blocks — we reduced from the standard 23 because pixel art geometry is simpler than photorealistic images. Then the features are upsampled twice using nearest-neighbor interpolation, giving us our 128 by 128 output."
"Two important design choices: no batch normalization in the generator to prevent artifacts, and residual scaling of 0.2 for stable training. Each RRDB block contains 3 Residual Dense Blocks, each with 5 convolutional layers densely connected."
"The discriminator is a standard VGG-style classifier with batch norm that progressively downsamples from 128 down to 4 and outputs a real/fake prediction."
6
Proposed Updates
▾
Update 1
"Our first update is the nearest-neighbor downsampling strategy we discussed. Standard SR models train on bicubic-downsampled images which are inherently blurry. By switching to NN downsampling, we teach the model that the LR-to-HR mapping is geometric, not smooth."
Update 2
"Our second update is the Edge-Aware Sharpness Loss. Standard perceptual loss using VGG encourages natural-looking textures like grass or skin. But for pixel art, we want the opposite — hard, binary edges."
"We compute Sobel gradients on the generated image. If the gradient magnitude falls in a middle range — indicating a smooth transition like anti-aliasing — we apply a penalty. This forces the network to output either flat regions or hard edges, nothing in between."
"Our total loss combines L1 pixel loss weighted at 1.0, LPIPS perceptual loss at 0.1, and our edge-aware loss at 0.5. We kept the adversarial weight at zero because it introduced noise with our limited training budget."
7
Training Results
▾
Say This
"We trained for 50 epochs with a batch size of 64 using mixed precision training for efficiency. We used Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 1e-4."
"Looking at the left chart — the generator loss in green converges very quickly, stabilizing after around epoch 10. The discriminator loss in red shows interesting oscillatory behavior — these spikes represent the discriminator re-adapting as the generator improves, which is normal in GAN training."
"The right chart breaks down the generator's loss components. L1 pixel loss dominates early training. LPIPS perceptual loss provides structural guidance. And notably, the edge-aware sharpness loss stays near zero throughout — meaning the model quickly learns to avoid anti-aliased edges."

Ablation Study
▾
Say This
"To validate the impact of our edge-aware loss, we ran an ablation study comparing three configurations: bicubic upscale, ESRGAN without edge loss, and ESRGAN with edge loss."
"The numbers tell a clear story. Bicubic gets a PSNR of only 23.18 and a terrible LPIPS of 0.48. Our ESRGAN without edge loss jumps to 41.51 PSNR with 0.004 LPIPS. And with the edge loss added, LPIPS improves further to 0.0029 — a 27% improvement in perceptual quality."
"There's a slight PSNR trade-off — 41.51 vs 41.10 — but this is expected. The edge loss encourages sharper outputs which can differ by a pixel or two from ground truth, slightly lowering PSNR while improving perceived quality. This is exactly what LPIPS captures."
10
Perceptual Quality — LPIPS
▾
Say This
"We want to highlight why LPIPS is the most important metric for this task. PSNR actually favors blurry images because it penalizes any pixel deviation equally. LPIPS, on the other hand, uses a learned perceptual model to measure how similar two images look to human eyes."
"The gap is dramatic. Our baseline bicubic has an LPIPS of 0.5082. Our Pixel-Perfect model achieves 0.0022. That's a 230 times improvement in perceptual quality. Essentially, our model's output is perceptually indistinguishable from the ground truth."
11
SOTA Comparison
▾
Say This
"For context, we compare against state-of-the-art results on Manga109, which serves as the closest proxy benchmark — it has sharp line art and clean boundaries."
"The best SOTA model, SwinIR trained on DF2K, achieves 32.03 dB PSNR and 0.926 SSIM on Manga109. Our model achieves 41.10 dB and 0.992 SSIM on our pixel art test set."
Important Caveat
"Now, we want to be transparent — this is not a direct comparison. Different datasets, different degradation models. Our higher numbers come from the fact that we're specialized for pixel art with NN degradation, which is inherently more structured than photorealistic SR. The key takeaway is: domain-specific adaptation significantly outperforms domain-general models."

Conclusion & Future Work
▾
Conclusion
"To summarize our key findings: domain-specific adaptation matters. By simply changing the downsampling strategy to nearest-neighbor and adding an edge-aware loss, we dramatically outperform generic upscaling. We achieved a PSNR of 41.10 dB, SSIM of 0.992, and LPIPS of 0.0029 — representing a 230x improvement over the bicubic baseline."
Future Work
"For future work, we'd like to:
Enable adversarial training with longer training to get even sharper textures
Add a palette-constraint loss to enforce the limited color palettes typical of retro games
Train on the full 89,000 images instead of our 5,000 subset
Explore using a SwinIR transformer backbone instead of the convolutional RRDB
And work toward real-time inference for integration into actual game emulators
