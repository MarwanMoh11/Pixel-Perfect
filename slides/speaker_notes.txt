SLIDE 2 — THE PROBLEM
Retro games like NES and SNES run at extremely low resolutions — around 256 by 240 pixels. When you display these on a modern 4K monitor, they need to be upscaled massively. Standard methods like bicubic interpolation just produce blurry, muddy results. And here's the interesting part — even state-of-the-art super-resolution models like SRGAN and SwinIR fail on pixel art. They're trained on photos, so they interpret those sharp pixel edges as noise and smooth them out. You get this "oil painting" effect that completely destroys the artistic intent. What we need instead is a model that respects pixel art geometry — hard edges, limited palettes, grid-aligned blocks.

---

SLIDE 3 — DATASET & PREPROCESSING
For our dataset, we selected the Kaggle Pixel Art Dataset — it contains about 89,000 pixel-art images of sprites and game objects, all at 16 by 16 resolution. We split this 80/10/10 for training, validation, and testing. The dataset is around 500 megabytes, which is well within Colab's capacity. A critical part of our preprocessing is the nearest-neighbor downsampling. We resize the sprites to 128 by 128 using NN interpolation to create our high-res targets, then downsample to 32 by 32 — also using NN — to create the low-res inputs. This preserves the blocky geometry that defines pixel art, unlike bicubic which introduces blur.

SLIDE 4 — SAMPLE PREPROCESSING PIPELINE
Here you can see actual samples from our preprocessing pipeline. ---

SLIDE 5 — MODEL ARCHITECTURE
Our model is based on ESRGAN — Enhanced Super-Resolution GAN. The generator uses the RRDBNet architecture — Residual-in-Residual Dense Block Network. The flow is: a 32 by 32 low-res image enters the network, passes through an initial convolution, then through 8 RRDB blocks — we reduced from the standard 23 because pixel art geometry is simpler than photorealistic images. Then the features are upsampled twice using nearest-neighbor interpolation, giving us our 128 by 128 output. Two important design choices: no batch normalization in the generator to prevent artifacts, and residual scaling of 0.2 for stable training. Each RRDB block contains 3 Residual Dense Blocks, each with 5 convolutional layers densely connected. The discriminator is a standard VGG-style classifier with batch norm that progressively downsamples from 128 down to 4 and outputs a real/fake prediction.

---

SLIDE 6 — PROPOSED UPDATES (MILESTONE 2 ADDITIONS)
Our first core update remains the nearest-neighbor downsampling strategy, which teaches the model that LR-to-HR mapping is geometric. For Milestone 2, we have expanded our second update. Alongside our Sobel-based Edge-Aware Sharpness Loss, which eliminates the "oily" look of standard models, we've implemented a new Palette-Constraint Loss. This loss penalizes generated colors that do not fall perfectly onto a discrete 8-bit or 16-bit color grid. This entirely prevents color drifting — a common issue where GANs invent new colors not present in the original sprite's limited palette. Our total loss now combines L1 pixel loss, LPIPS perceptual loss, our edge loss, the new palette loss, and we have fully enabled Adversarial Loss to push texture sharpness to the maximum.

---

SLIDE 7 — MILESTONE 2 PROGRESS & FINAL RESULTS
For this final milestone, we scaled to the full 89,399-image dataset and trained for 20 epochs with mixed-precision (AMP) on a Colab T4 GPU. Looking at the charts, the discriminator loss collapses toward zero by epoch 14, meaning the generator has learned to produce outputs that perfectly fool the discriminator — exactly what we want for pixel art where the output should be indistinguishable from real sprites. The adversarial loss component dominates the total generator loss, driving texture sharpness, while our edge and palette losses remain low and stable — proving our custom constraints guide the GAN without destabilizing training. This full-scale training is the core achievement of Milestone 2.

SLIDE 9 — ABLATION STUDY
To validate the impact of our edge-aware loss, we ran an ablation study comparing three configurations: bicubic upscale, ESRGAN without edge loss, and ESRGAN with edge loss. The numbers tell a clear story. Bicubic gets a PSNR of only 23.18 and a terrible LPIPS of 0.48. Our ESRGAN without edge loss jumps to 41.51 PSNR with 0.004 LPIPS. And with the edge loss added, LPIPS improves further to 0.0029 — a 27% improvement in perceptual quality. There's a slight PSNR trade-off — 41.51 vs 41.10 — but this is expected. The edge loss encourages sharper outputs which can differ by a pixel or two from ground truth, slightly lowering PSNR while improving perceived quality. This is exactly what LPIPS captures.

---

SLIDE 10 — PERCEPTUAL QUALITY (LPIPS)
We want to highlight why LPIPS is the most important metric for this task. PSNR actually favors blurry images because it penalizes any pixel deviation equally. LPIPS, on the other hand, uses a learned perceptual model to measure how similar two images look to human eyes. The gap is dramatic. Our baseline bicubic has an LPIPS of 0.4874. Our Pixel-Perfect model achieves 0.0062. That's a 79 times improvement in perceptual quality. Essentially, our model's output is perceptually indistinguishable from the ground truth.

---

SLIDE 11 — SOTA COMPARISON
For context, we compare against state-of-the-art results on Manga109, which serves as the closest proxy benchmark — it has sharp line art and clean boundaries. The best SOTA model, SwinIR trained on DF2K, achieves 32.03 dB PSNR and 0.926 SSIM on Manga109. Our model achieves 41.10 dB and 0.992 SSIM on our pixel art test set. Now, we want to be transparent — this is not a direct comparison. Different datasets, different degradation models. Our higher numbers come from the fact that we're specialized for pixel art with NN degradation, which is inherently more structured than photorealistic SR. The key takeaway is: domain-specific adaptation significantly outperforms domain-general models.

- Why does the discriminator loss collapse to zero? -> This is expected in pixel art SR — the output becomes near-identical to ground truth, so the discriminator can't distinguish real from generated. It means the generator has converged well.
- Why did you resume from epoch 6? -> Our Colab GPU quota expired mid-training. We implemented crash-resilient checkpointing that saves full training state every epoch, so we resumed seamlessly from the epoch 5 checkpoint.
- How does it handle transparency? -> We crop to content first, removing black/transparent borders before processing.
- Why 79× and not higher? -> The 79× LPIPS improvement is measured against bicubic. Our model already achieves near-zero LPIPS (0.0062), meaning further gains require sub-pixel precision.
