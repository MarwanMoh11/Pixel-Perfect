Abstract

Retro video game art is defined by hard-edged, grid-aligned pixels with limited color palettes. Standard super-resolution models, trained on photorealistic data, interpret these sharp boundaries as noise and smooth them out — producing an unacceptable "oil painting" effect. Pixel-Perfect introduces a specialized ESRGAN-based model that respects the geometric constraints of pixel art. By combining Nearest-Neighbor training pairs, a novel Edge-Aware Sharpness Loss, and a Palette-Constraint Loss, we achieve a 79x improvement in perceptual quality (LPIPS) over standard bicubic upscaling while preserving the artistic intent of the original sprites.

Problem Statement

Why Standard Upscaling Fails

  • Retro games run at ~256×240 resolution — tiny on modern 4K monitors.
  • Bicubic/Bilinear interpolation produces blurry, muddy images.
  • State-of-the-art SR models (SRGAN, SwinIR) are trained on photorealistic data and interpret pixel edges as noise.
  • Result: an "oil painting" effect that destroys the artistic intent.

What We Need

  • Sharp, binary edges — no anti-aliasing.
  • Limited color palettes preserved exactly.
  • Grid-aligned pixel geometry respected.
  • 4× resolution increase (32×32 → 128×128).

Dataset

Kaggle Pixel Art Dataset

  • 89,399 total pixel-art images (sprites & objects).
  • All images are 16×16 resolution.
  • 80/10/10 Train / Validation / Test split.
  • 71,519 train · 8,939 val · 8,941 test.

Nearest-Neighbor Preprocessing

Unlike standard SR pipelines that use bicubic downsampling (which introduces blur), we create all training pairs using Nearest-Neighbor downsampling. This preserves blocky pixel geometry and teaches the model that the LR→HR mapping is geometric, not smooth.

Key Design Choices

  • No Batch Normalization in Generator — prevents artifacts.
  • Residual scaling (0.2) for stable deep training.
  • Dense connections within each block (5 conv layers).
  • 8 RRDB blocks — sufficient for pixel-art geometry.

VGG-Style Discriminator

  • Processes 128×128 patches.
  • 10 conv layers with BatchNorm + LeakyReLU.
  • Progressive downsampling: 128→64→32→16→8→4.
  • Binary classifier: Real vs. Generated.

Custom Loss Functions

Edge-Aware Sharpness Loss

A Sobel-based penalty that targets the "smooth transition" magnitudes characteristic of anti-aliasing. This forces the generator to output hard, binary edges instead of gradients — eliminating the "oily" look of standard SR outputs.

Palette-Constraint Loss

A novel penalty that snaps generated color values to discrete 8-bit/16-bit color grids. This prevents the GAN from "inventing" new colors not present in the original sprite's limited palette — a common issue called color drifting.

Why LPIPS Matters Most

  • PSNR favors blurry outputs — it penalizes any pixel deviation equally.
  • LPIPS measures perceptual similarity — how humans actually perceive quality.
  • Our model: 0.0029 LPIPS (near-perfect perceptual fidelity).
  • Baseline: 0.4758 LPIPS.
  • That's a 79× improvement in human-perceived quality.

Impact of Edge-Aware Loss

To validate the impact of our custom loss, we compared three configurations:

  • Adding the Edge-Aware loss improves LPIPS by 27% (0.0040 → 0.0029).
  • There is a slight PSNR trade-off (41.51 → 41.10) — expected because sharpening can shift pixels by ±1.
  • This trade-off is precisely captured by LPIPS: sharper outputs look better to humans even if PSNR drops slightly.
